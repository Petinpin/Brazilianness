Overview

A Nation of Superlatives

Brazil is the largest country in South America and the fifth largest in the world by both area and population. As the only Portuguese-speaking nation in the Americas, it occupies a unique position in global culture, economics, and ecology. From the colonial era beginning in the 1500s, through independence in 1822, to rapid industrialization in the twentieth century, Brazil has forged a rich multicultural identity blending indigenous, African, European, and Asian heritages.

Economically, Brazil is a major agricultural exporter — leading global exports of coffee, soybeans, and sugarcane — and ranks among the world's top ten economies. With over 215 million inhabitants speaking dozens of indigenous languages alongside Portuguese, Brazil embodies remarkable human diversity set against some of the planet's most breathtaking natural landscapes.

About This Project

My Ties to Brazil

São Paulo state interior — the region where Peterson was born and raised in Jundiaí. 📷 Unsplash

Peterson Pinheiro was born and raised in Jundiaí, São Paulo, a city nestled in the interior of Brazil's most populous and economically dynamic state. Growing up in the interior of São Paulo exposed him to the everyday rhythms of Brazilian life — from the rolling hills of the Atlantic Forest transition zone surrounding Jundiaí to the surrounding agricultural landscapes of sugarcane and vineyards for which the region is known. This project is a personal exploration of the land that shaped him, an opportunity to understand the geological, climatic, and hydrological forces that created the country he calls home.

One Fascinating Geographic Fact

Brazil is home to the Amazon River basin, which drains approximately 40% of South America and contains nearly one-fifth of all fresh water flowing into the world's oceans. The river stretches over 6,400 km from the Peruvian Andes to the Atlantic Ocean, and during flood season its width can exceed 50 km — so wide that from the middle of the river you cannot see either bank.

Maps, Location & Cartographers

Maps tell the story of how people understood, claimed, and navigated Brazil across centuries. Below are four maps that reveal Brazil's geographic position, colonial history, territorial evolution, and modern boundaries.

Map 1

The Cantino Planisphere (1502)

One of the earliest known maps to depict the Brazilian coastline, drawn just two years after Pedro Álvares Cabral's expedition. Secretly smuggled out of Portugal by an Italian spy, it shows the coast of "Terra da Vera Cruz" — Brazil's original name — with a line representing the Tordesillas Treaty boundary between Portuguese and Spanish colonial claims.

Map 2

Modern Political Map — Mercator Projection

This interactive map uses the Mercator projection (Gerardus Mercator, 1569). It shows Brazil's territory and borders with 10 of South America's 12 countries — all except Ecuador and Chile. Brazil covers 8.5 million km², making it the world's fifth-largest country. You can zoom and pan. Source: OpenStreetMap contributors.

Map 3

Continental Position & Latitudinal Range

This map shows Brazil's position within South America. Brazil's northernmost point (Cabo Orange, Amapá) sits at 5°16'N and its southernmost point (Arroio Chuí, Rio Grande do Sul) at 33°45'S — a range of nearly 39 degrees of latitude, explaining Brazil's extraordinary biome diversity. Source: OpenStreetMap contributors.

Map 4

Topographic & Relief Map

This topographic view shows Brazil's elevation. The vast lowlands of the Amazon Basin in the north contrast with the rugged Brazilian Highlands in the central plateau and southeastern coastline. Understanding this topographic division is key to explaining the distribution of Brazil's six major biomes. Source: OpenStreetMap/CyclOSM.

Location Questions

Geography at a Glance

  • On which continent and in what general area is Brazil located? Brazil is located in South America, occupying the central and eastern portion of the continent. It borders every South American country except Ecuador and Chile, and its eastern coastline faces the Atlantic Ocean. Brazil's vast territory covers roughly half of South America's total land area.
  • What is the latitudinal range of Brazil? Brazil spans from approximately 5°16'N (Cabo Orange, Amapá) in the north to 33°45'S (Arroio Chuí, Rio Grande do Sul) in the south — a total latitudinal range of nearly 39 degrees. In longitude, Brazil extends from roughly 34°47'W to 73°59'W. This equatorial location means large portions receive intense solar radiation year-round, sustaining the Amazon rainforest.
  • During what time periods or years has Brazil existed? Indigenous peoples have inhabited present-day Brazil for at least 12,000 years. European colonization began in 1500 when Pedro Álvares Cabral arrived. Colonial administration was formalized in the 1530s through hereditary captaincies. Brazil declared independence on September 7, 1822, becoming the Empire of Brazil under Dom Pedro I, and became a federal republic on November 15, 1889, a status it retains today.
  • Are there any famous cartographers who mapped Brazil? Yes — see the profile below of João Teixeira Albernaz I, the most prolific Portuguese cartographer of the seventeenth century, whose work extensively documented colonial Brazil.

Portuguese Cartographer

João Teixeira Albernaz I is recognized as the most prolific Portuguese cartographer of the seventeenth century. Born into a cartographic dynasty — his father Luís Teixeira was also a royal cosmographer — João was formally trained from a young age. In 1602, he received the title of "Master of nautical charts, astrolabes, compass needles, and cross staff," and was appointed cartographer to the Casa da Índia, the Portuguese body that administered colonial trade (Neatline Maps, n.d.).

Over his career, Albernaz produced 19 atlases containing 215 maps (Wikipedia, 2024). His 1640 Atlas of Brazil consisted of 32 sheets documenting the coastline, river mouths, islands, ports, and fortifications of the entire Brazilian coast (Wikipedia, 2024).

His biggest cartographic accomplishments:

  • The 1630 Taboas Geraes de Toda a Navegação — a 32-sheet world atlas held at the Library of Congress, serving as the official Portuguese royal atlas of the era (Library of Congress, n.d.).
  • The 1640 Atlas of Brazil, the most detailed cartographic record of colonial Brazil at the time, documenting all captaincies along the Atlantic coast (Wikipedia, 2024).
  • His work informed political negotiations over colonial boundaries, including the Treaty of Madrid (1750) (Bahia.ws, n.d.).

Albernaz worked in an era when Portuguese cartographic knowledge was treated as a state secret — maps were handwritten to prevent rivals from accessing geographic intelligence. As one scholar notes, "Portuguese cartography has to do with the mode of colonization: commercial, pragmatic and economic" (IBGE News Agency, 2019).
