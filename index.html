Historical · Cultural · Economic Overview

A Nation of Superlatives

Brazil is the largest country in South America and the fifth largest in the world by size and population. It is the only Portuguese-speaking nation in the Americas and holds a unique place in global culture, economics, and ecology. From the colonial era of the 1500s through independence in 1822, and rapid industrialization and urbanization of the 1900s, Brazil has forged a rich multicultural identity blending indigenous, African, European, and Asian heritages.

Economically, Brazil is a powerhouse in agriculture (home of the world's largest exports of coffee, soybeans, and sugarcane) and ranks as the world's 10th-largest economy. With over 200 endemic sovereign nations, Brazil is also home to one of the world's most vibrant student cultures — famous for futebol, samba, carnival, and its world-renewing capacity.

Its social landscape is shaped by deep inequality alongside vibrant community life. With over 215 million inhabitants speaking dozens of indigenous languages alongside Portuguese, Brazil represents remarkable biodiversity set against breathtaking natural landscapes.

São Paulo

São Paulo — Cosmopolitan Capital

Economic Capital

São Paulo is the most populous city in the Southern Hemisphere and is home to approximately 12 million residents in the city proper and over 20 million in the greater metropolitan area, making it one of the largest urban agglomerations on Earth.

The city's growth from a small Jesuit settlement in 1554 to a massive 20th-century transformation is a testament to relentless ambition. São Paulo is one of the key economic and cultural heart of Brazil — a city of extreme contrasts: gleaming skyscrapers meet hillside favelas; Michelin-starred restaurants neighbor open-air markets.

Today São Paulo is among the world's top 10 economic cities, with a metro area GDP of over $400 billion USD, and the city serves as a hub for finance, fashion, art, and gastronomy.

Amazon Rainforest

Brazil contains approximately 60% of the Amazon Rainforest, covering about 3.9 million km². The Amazon is the world's largest tropical rainforest, covering about 5.5 million km², containing about 10% of all species on Earth, and generating approximately 20% of the world's oxygen.

02 · Wetland

Pantanal

The world's largest tropical wetland area, at approximately 150,000–195,000 km², is located in west-central Brazil. It is home to over 1,000 bird species, 400 fish species, and extraordinary wildlife including jaguars and giant otters.

Cerrado

Brazil's vast tropical savanna is the world's most biodiverse savanna ecosystem. Known as the "cradle of waters," the Cerrado feeds eight of Brazil's major river basins and is home to a staggering diversity of endemic plants and wildlife species.

Hydrology

The Rivers of Brazil

Brazil contains approximately 12% of the world's available fresh water, flowing through some of the planet's most remarkable river systems.

Amazon River

Known as the "River Sea," the Amazon discharges about 20% of all fresh water flowing into the world's oceans. It flows 6,400 km from the Andes to the Atlantic, carrying more water than any other river on Earth.

Paraná River

South America's second-longest river at nearly 4,880 km, the Paraná flows through Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina. The Itaipu Dam on the Paraná is one of the world's largest hydroelectric power plants.

São Francisco River

Known as the "River of National Unity," it flows 2,900 km through northeastern and eastern Brazil, providing water to millions in one of the driest regions of the country.
