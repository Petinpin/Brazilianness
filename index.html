Overview

A Nation of Superlatives

Brazil is the largest country in South America and the fifth largest in the world by both area and population. As the only Portuguese-speaking nation in the Americas, it occupies a unique position in global culture, economics, and ecology. From the colonial era beginning in the 1500s, through independence in 1822, to rapid industrialization in the twentieth century, Brazil has forged a rich multicultural identity blending indigenous, African, European, and Asian heritages.

Economically, Brazil is a major agricultural exporter — leading global exports of coffee, soybeans, and sugarcane — and ranks among the world's top ten economies. With over 215 million inhabitants speaking dozens of indigenous languages alongside Portuguese, Brazil embodies remarkable human diversity set against some of the planet's most breathtaking natural landscapes.

About This Project

My Ties to Brazil

Jundiaí, São Paulo — the city in the interior of Brazil where Peterson was born and raised.

Peterson Pinheiro was born and raised in Jundiaí, São Paulo, a city nestled in the interior of Brazil's most populous and economically dynamic state. Growing up in the interior of São Paulo exposed him to the everyday rhythms of Brazilian life — from the rolling hills of the Atlantic Forest transition zone surrounding Jundiaí to the surrounding agricultural landscapes of sugarcane and vineyards for which the region is known. This project is a personal exploration of the land that shaped him, an opportunity to understand the geological, climatic, and hydrological forces that created the country he calls home.

One Fascinating Geographic Fact

Brazil is home to the Amazon River basin, which drains approximately 40% of South America and contains nearly one-fifth of all the fresh water flowing into the world's oceans. The river itself stretches over 6,400 km from the Peruvian Andes to the Atlantic Ocean, and during flood season, the Amazon's width can exceed 50 km in certain areas — so wide that from the middle of the river you cannot see either bank.

Maps, Location & Cartographers

Maps tell the story of how people understood, claimed, and navigated Brazil across centuries. Below are four maps that together reveal Brazil's geographic position, colonial history, territorial evolution, and modern boundaries.

Map 1

The Cantino Planisphere (1502)

This is one of the earliest known maps to depict the Brazilian coastline, drawn just two years after Pedro Álvares Cabral's Portuguese expedition reached the territory in 1500. The map was secretly smuggled out of Portugal by an Italian spy and shows the coast of "Terra da Vera Cruz" — Brazil's original name — with a green line representing the Tordesillas Treaty boundary dividing Portuguese and Spanish colonial claims. It demonstrates how strategically sensitive geographic knowledge was in the age of exploration.

Map 2

Modern Political Map — Mercator Projection

This contemporary interactive map uses the Mercator projection, developed by Flemish cartographer Gerardus Mercator in 1569. It displays Brazil's territory and borders with 10 of South America's 12 countries (all except Ecuador and Chile). Brazil occupies the central-eastern portion of the South American continent, spanning from approximately 5°N to 33°S latitude and covering 8.5 million km² — making it the world's fifth-largest country by area. Map source: OpenStreetMap contributors.

Map 3

Continental Position & Latitudinal Range

This map shows Brazil's position within South America, illustrating its equatorial to subtropical latitudinal range. Brazil straddles the equator, with its northernmost point (Cabo Orange, Amapá) at approximately 5°16'N and its southernmost point (Arroio Chuí, Rio Grande do Sul) at approximately 33°45'S — a range of nearly 39 degrees of latitude. This accounts for the extraordinary diversity of Brazil's biomes, from Amazonian rainforest near the equator to temperate grasslands in the far south. Map source: OpenStreetMap contributors.

Map 4

Brazil's Six Major Biomes — Satellite View

This satellite view reveals the distribution of Brazil's six recognized biomes: the Amazon rainforest (covering ~49% of the country), the Cerrado savanna (~23%), the Caatinga semi-arid scrubland (~10%), the Atlantic Forest (only ~13% of its original extent remains), the Pantanal wetlands (~2%), and the Pampa grasslands (~2%). The dark green of the Amazon in the north and west contrasts clearly with the lighter tones of the Cerrado in the central plateau. Map source: Google Maps.

Location Questions

Geography at a Glance

  • On which continent and in what general area is Brazil located? Brazil is located in South America, occupying the central and eastern portion of the continent. It borders every South American country except Ecuador and Chile, and its eastern coastline faces the Atlantic Ocean. Brazil's vast territory covers roughly half of South America's total land area.
  • What is the latitudinal range of Brazil? Brazil spans from approximately 5°16'N (Cabo Orange, Amapá, in the north) to 33°45'S (Arroio Chuí, Rio Grande do Sul, in the south), giving it a total latitudinal range of nearly 39 degrees. In terms of longitude, Brazil extends from roughly 34°47'W to 73°59'W. The country's equatorial location means that large portions receive intense solar radiation year-round, directly influencing the growth of the Amazon rainforest.
  • During what time periods or years has Brazil existed? Indigenous peoples have inhabited the territory of present-day Brazil for at least 12,000 years, with some archaeological evidence suggesting human presence as far back as 50,000 years ago. European colonization began in 1500 when Portuguese navigator Pedro Álvares Cabral arrived. Portugal formally established colonial administration in the 1530s, dividing the territory into hereditary captaincies. Brazil declared independence from Portugal on September 7, 1822, becoming the Empire of Brazil under Dom Pedro I. It became a federal republic on November 15, 1889, following the abolition of slavery in 1888, and has remained a republic to the present day.
  • Are there any famous cartographers who mapped Brazil? Yes — see the section below for a detailed profile of João Teixeira Albernaz I, the most prolific Portuguese cartographer of the seventeenth century, whose work extensively documented colonial Brazil.
Cartographic History

João Teixeira Albernaz I (c. late 1500s – c. 1662)

Map of Brazil by João Teixeira Albernaz I, 1640. Public domain via Wikimedia Commons.

Portuguese Cartographer

João Teixeira Albernaz I is widely recognized as the most prolific Portuguese cartographer of the seventeenth century. Born into a cartographic dynasty — his father Luís Teixeira was also a royal cosmographer — João was formally trained in the craft from a young age. In 1602, he was examined by the Royal Cosmographer João Batista Lavanha and received the title of "Master of nautical charts, astrolabes, compass needles, and cross staff." Three years later, he was appointed cartographer to the Casa da Índia (India House), the Portuguese state body that regulated international trade and colonial administration (Neatline Maps, n.d.).

Over the course of his career, Albernaz produced an extraordinary body of work: 19 atlases containing a total of 215 maps (Wikipedia, 2024). His focus on Brazil was unparalleled among cartographers of the era. His 1640 Atlas of Brazil — formally titled Descripção de todo o Marítimo da Terra de Santa Cruz chamado vulgarmente o Brasil — consisted of 32 sheets documenting the coastline, river mouths, islands, ports, and fortifications of the entire Brazilian coast, with each map preceded by an explanatory text sheet (Wikipedia, 2024).

His biggest cartographic accomplishments include:

  • His 1630 Taboas Geraes de Toda a Navegação — a comprehensive 32-sheet manuscript world atlas held at the Library of Congress in Washington, D.C., which served as the official Portuguese royal atlas of the era (Library of Congress, n.d.).
  • The 1640 Atlas of Brazil, considered the most detailed cartographic record of colonial Brazil up to that time, documenting all captaincies along the Atlantic coast (Wikipedia, 2024).
  • His work helped establish Brazilian geography as understood in Europe, informing later cartographers and political negotiations over colonial boundaries, including those surrounding the Treaty of Madrid (1750) (Bahia.ws, n.d.).

Albernaz worked during a politically charged era in which Portuguese cartographic knowledge was treated as a state secret — maps were handwritten, not printed, to prevent rivals from accessing geographic intelligence. As one scholar explains, "Portuguese cartography has to do with the mode of colonization: commercial, pragmatic and economic" (IBGE News Agency, 2019). Albernaz's maps were part of this strategic project of territorial documentation and defense.
